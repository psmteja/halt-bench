Abstract

As language-model agents are deployed in increasingly consequential workflows, evaluation remains disproportionately focused on task completion, final-answer correctness, and broad tool-use competence. These metrics are insufficient in settings where the key deployment question is not merely whether an agent can solve a task, but whether it can determine when to act, when to gather more evidence, and when to escalate to a human.

We introduce HALT, a benchmark for evaluating bounded agentic decision-making under partial observability, constrained tools, and explicit escalation options. HALT is grounded in defensive cybersecurity workflows — where acting too early, failing to escalate, or over-escalating can all be costly — and contains 1,248 instances across four decision regimes, balanced at 312 per class. We evaluate 16 systems and find that tool access improves evidence gathering substantially more than escalation correctness, and that stronger agents often improve final task success without reliably improving stop-or-escalate judgment. HALT exposes a deployment-critical gap between capability and bounded judgment.

Benchmark Design — §2–3

Four Decision Regimes & Latent State

Each instance is governed by a latent state triple (σ, ρ, α): evidence sufficiency, uncertainty recoverability, and action authorization. The correct label follows deterministically.

01
ACT NOW

Act Now

Available evidence sufficient. σ=1, α=1.

02
INVESTIGATE

Investigate Further

Evidence insufficient but ρ=1: a tool step can help. σ=0, ρ=1, α=1.

03
ESCALATE

Escalate to Human

Irrecoverable uncertainty or authority gap. σ=0, ρ=0.

04
REFUSE

Refuse / Block

Request is unsafe or out-of-policy. unsafe=1.

# Latent state triple governing each instance (§2)
state = (σ, ρ, α) # sufficiency · recoverability · authorization

y* = ACT iff σ=1 ∧ α=1
y* = INV iff σ=0 ∧ ρ=1 ∧ α=1
y* = ESC iff σ=0 ∧ (ρ=0 ∨ α=0)
y* = REF iff unsafe=1

Calibration Analysis

Uncertainty Calibration & Overreach–Deferral Trade-off

Systems are required to report a confidence score c ∈ [0,1]. We evaluate calibration via ECE and Brier score, and the overreach–deferral trade-off reveals a fundamental deployment dilemma.

Figure · Overreach vs. Deferral scatter — all systems
Each point = one system. The Pareto frontier (lower-left) is unachieved by any system. Human analyst is the only point satisfying both UOR≤10% and ODR≤12%.
Figure. UOR (unsafe overreach) vs ODR (over-deferral) for all 17 evaluated systems. Red dashed lines show deployment gates (UOR=10%, ODR=12%). The human analyst (★) is the only point in the safe quadrant (bottom-left).
Figure · ECE (Expected Calibration Error) by system
Lower ECE = better calibration. Safety-oriented systems are better calibrated but over-deferring. Aggressive systems are overconfident.

Ablation Study

Four Targeted Ablations

We validate benchmark design choices through four controlled ablations. Results confirm that stopping-aware scoring, authority constraints, and adversarial resistance are all essential — and that benchmark conclusions are robust across composite weighting choices.

Ablation 1 · Stopping-aware vs. completion-only scoring
Replacing trajectory-aware scoring with final-answer-only narrows the gap between tool agents and single-shot LLMs by 6.2 pts on average.
Ablation 1. Mean DBA under stopping-aware (default) vs. completion-only scoring, by system class. The 6.2-pt narrowing confirms that conventional completion metrics systematically understate the importance of the handoff decision.
Ablation 3 · Adversarial pressure prompts → UOR increase
Phrases like "make your best guess" increase UOR most strongly for reflection-heavy and multi-agent systems (+5.9 pts).
Ablation 3. UOR increase under adversarial pressure prompts, by system class. Multi-agent systems are most susceptible (+5.9 pts), consistent with persistence amplifying overreach when the stopping boundary is weakly represented.
Ablation 2 · Effect of removing authority constraints (α)
Removing α improves all systems on ACT and INV cases but eliminates the most realistic ESC instances. Benchmark diagnostic value drops sharply.
Ablation 2. DBA change (±pp) when removing authority constraints from ESC-labeled instances. ACT/INV accuracy improves (agents no longer penalized for authority overreach) but ESC accuracy collapses — those instances disappear from the benchmark.
Ablation 4 · Rank stability under alternative EscScore weights
Kendall's W = 0.91 over 100 alternative weight vectors. Top-3 systems retain positions in 94% of rankings.
Ablation 4. System rank stability across 100 random EscScore weight vectors sampled from the simplex. Kendall's W=0.91 indicates high consistency. Rankings change most when ODR penalty exceeds 0.4 — an operationally unrealistic regime.
Ablation summary: (1) Stopping-aware scoring is essential — completion-only metrics understate handoff importance by 6.2 pts. (2) Authority constraints are essential — removing them eliminates the most realistic ESC cases. (3) Adversarial pressure increases UOR most for multi-agent systems. (4) Benchmark conclusions are robust (W=0.91) across composite-weight variation.

Case Study — §5

Capability Without Judgment: ESC-hard-047

A representative forensic-reconstruction case. Gold label: ESC. Every automated system fails despite assembling coherent narratives. Only the human analyst correctly identifies irrecoverable uncertainty.

Instance ESC-hard-047 · privileged-access anomaly
Behavior spread across system classes
Three properties exposed: (1) Factual plausibility ≠ correct stopping decision. (2) Richer scaffolds improve evidence coverage without improving stop-or-escalate judgment. (3) The benchmark rewards recognizing irrecoverable uncertainty — not merely appearing competent. An agent that confidently continues is operationally less trustworthy than one that escalates.
